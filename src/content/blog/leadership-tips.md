## Be open that you don't know everything

There is a lot of face saving in software engineering; I've seen scenarios where everyone is expected to know every single thing or they're not a 'real' engineer. It can be an incredibly toxic place to be, created and sustained from insecurity. I once had a chat with a former junior-level report who said they don't like to ask for help because they were embarrassed to do so. They also didn't feel comfortable speaking in our internal web space because "they couldn't teach anything to anyone at a higher level". I have seen this a lot and experienced it myself; in my first job I was scolded by another dev for not knowing something 'obvious'. It's a bit of a cliche, but asking for help shouldn't be a weakness, it should be an sign of growth.

I make a point to admit to other developers when there's something I don't know, or that I had to Google something I had forgotten. I like to think anyone can learn anything from anyone, and I don't like spaces where there are the seniors at the top of the tree looking down their noses at the juniors - everyone has something to contribute and they were juniors once themselves! When I was a junior it was overwhelming to see how some people seemed to know everything and I knew barely anything in comparison, so I try to be the person I needed back then.

## Get to know your team personally

You can make mass-generalised assumptions of what you think is right for your team, but if you don't even know your team, then how can you correctly make those assumptions? I have seen autocratic decisions made that destroyed a team's confidence and morale because _they_ had the keys to the castle.

Getting to know everyone in your team is an imperative part to being a good leader; knowing who you are serving and what their needs are will help you both achieve their goals, which is such an important part of being a leader - servant leadership.

## Consistently look for opportuntities for others

This should be second-nature to leaders - constantly look for opportunity for your team to gain experience from, whether it be project-focused or company-wide. Is there a small task that needs doing? Great, see if someone in your team would benefit from doing that. Is there an important topic or bit of industry news worth sharing? Maybe someone else who needs a confidence boost can do that. Giving someone else an opportunity to present themselves in the limelight, especially if the eyes of the bosses are watching, can change the course for someone's career. If nobody knows what you do, nobody knows why you should be promoted.
